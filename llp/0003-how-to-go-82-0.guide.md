## What this is

This is the friendly version of the strategy — the story of what the Coach is
*thinking* when it tells you to take a player, spend a skip, or start over. The exact
formulas, constants, and the simulations that justify them live in
**[LLP 0001](./0001-82-0-team-strategy.spec.md)**. If a number here ever disagrees with a
number there, **0001 wins** — this document is the map, not the territory.

## The mountain you're climbing

82-0 means a *perfect* season: all 82 wins, zero losses. To project that, your five
players' combined stats have to push your team's overall rating (teamOVR) up to **109.5**.

The Coach boils each player down to a single number it calls **`val`** — basically "how
much does this player move my teamOVR?" High scorers who also rebound, pass, steal, and
block score high; role players score low. The useful fact is that `val` is *additive*: to
reach a perfect season your five players' `val`s need to add up to about **108**.

Do the division and you get the whole problem in one line: **you need to average about 21
`val` per slot.** Almost nobody puts up a 21 — those are star seasons (Jokić, Giannis,
Jordan, Luka). You cannot get there by taking five *pretty good* players. You need real
stars, in the right positions, and the machine hands them out at random. So the game isn't
really about picking — it's about **knowing when to walk away and spin again.**

## The whole strategy in one sentence

> **Be ruthless early, patient late.**

Restarting is free and instant; a wasted slot is forever. So the Coach throws away weak
starts without a second thought, then guards a good start like treasure.

## Playing it out

**Round 1 — the anchor.** The first player sets the ceiling for the whole run. The Coach
says *take* only if the best available is a genuine star — **`val` ≥ 21**. Anything less
and it says **restart**. This feels wasteful the first hundred times you do it, but a
Round-1 restart costs you a single spin and keeps *both* your skips in your pocket for the
hard part later. Don't fall in love with a decent opener. The mountain is too steep to
start halfway down.

**Rounds 2–4 — the climb.** Now you're building. Take the best player who fits an open
slot, as long as they clear **`val` ≥ 17**. If the whole pool is junk — nobody worth a
slot — spend a skip and spin a fresher one; that's exactly what skips are for. And there's
a gut-check at the midpoint: **if your total `val` hasn't cleared 40 after two players, the
run is already behind**, and the Coach pulls the plug rather than chase a lost cause up the
back nine.

**Round 5 — the close.** The final slot is pass/fail. The Coach says *take* **only if that
player actually finishes you at 82-0.** If it doesn't and you still have a skip, spin for
the missing piece. If you're out of skips and can't get there, grab someone to end the game
and start fresh — this run simply wasn't the one. No shame in it; most runs aren't.

## Your two skips

Each game gives you exactly two re-rolls, and once they're spent they're gone:

- a **Team skip** — keep the decade, swap to a different team, and
- an **Era skip** — keep the team, swap to a different decade.

The Coach **hoards them early** (that's why Round 1 restarts instead of skipping) and
**spends them in the middle**, when a single weak pool is all that's standing between you
and a strong roster. Its rule of thumb: if you're stuck in a *thin* era — the **1980s and
2000s** are the shallowest at the top — burn the **Era** skip to escape the decade
entirely. Otherwise, change the **team** first.

## Where to put people

There are five slots — **PG, SG, SF, PF, C** — and a player can only go where they're
eligible. When someone fits more than one open slot, the Coach drops them into the
**scarcest** one first, working down the order **SG → PG → SF → PF → C**.

The reasoning is supply and demand: good big men (PF, C) turn up fairly often, but elite
guards and wings are rare. So you grab the scarce guard/wing slots the moment a star can
fill one, and you leave the flexible big-man spots open for later. (Where you place a player
doesn't change your score — it only affects who you can still fit. It's a packing problem,
not a scoring one.)

## The counterintuitive truth

Here's the part that surprises everyone. The Coach is tuned to get you to your *first* 82-0
in the **fewest spins** — the least time standing at the machine — **not** to win as often
as possible. Those turn out to be *different goals*.

The tuned Coach actually completes a perfect season slightly *less* often per attempt than
a looser strategy would… and yet it gets you there **faster**. Why? Because bailing on a
bad start costs **one** spin, while grinding a doomed roster all the way to a 96-win finish
wastes **five or six**. Fold early, fold often, and pounce only when the draw is real.

Plan on roughly **90 attempts — about 195 spins — to land your first 82-0.** It's hard.
That's the game.

## Things that sound smart but aren't

We didn't guess at the strategy — a simulator played *millions* of games to settle it (see
[LLP 0001 → Tuning](./0001-82-0-team-strategy.spec.md#tuning)). Two tempting ideas that the
data killed:

- **"Bail the instant a run looks doomed."** Doesn't help. By the time a run is *provably*
  hopeless, you've already spent the spins — and pulling out early sometimes kills a run
  that would've made it. It's a wash, so the Coach doesn't do mid-run doom-bailing.
- **"Hold out for an even bigger star to anchor."** Backfires. Raise the bar past `val` 21
  and you restart so much waiting for a unicorn that you lose more time than you save. **21
  is the sweet spot.**

## The cheat sheet

| When | Do this |
|---|---|
| **Round 1** | Take **only a star** (`val` ≥ 21). Otherwise **restart** and keep both skips. |
| **Rounds 2–4** | Take the best fit (`val` ≥ 17). Weak pool → **skip**. Behind pace at the midpoint (total < 40) → **restart**. |
| **Round 5** | Take **only if it finishes you at 82-0**. Otherwise **skip**, or restart. |
| **Skips** | Hoard early, spend in the middle. **Era-skip** out of the 1980s / 2000s. |
| **Positions** | Fill the **scarcest** open slot first — guards and wings before big men. |
| **Mindset** | **Ruthless early, patient late.** Restarts are cheap; doomed runs are expensive. |
